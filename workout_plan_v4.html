Training
Program

Four sessions cycling through two upper and two lower body days. Upper/lower structure allows 48+ hours of tissue-specific recovery between sessions of the same type. Three sessions per week rotating sequentially through all four. Band anchor points sequenced for minimal friction within each session. HR curve managed deliberately across each circuit.

How the rotation works: Cycle through sessions 1→2→3→4→1→2→3… regardless of the day of the week. The critical rule: never run two upper or two lower sessions on consecutive days. Upper/Lower or Lower/Upper back-to-back is fine. On a 3-session week the sequence shifts by one each week — over four weeks every session receives equal exposure. The 4th day each week is a dedicated mobility/active recovery session and does not consume a resistance slot.

Warm-Up — 3–4 min
Arm Circles Warmup ►
15 sec forward, 15 sec backward
Lubricates the shoulder joint through its full range of motion and increases blood flow to the rotator cuff before any pushing or pulling. Start small and progressively widen the circle.
How To
  • Stand with feet hip-width apart, arms extended straight out to your sides
  • Draw circles with your fingertips — start small and gradually widen to full range
  • 15 seconds forward, then immediately reverse for 15 seconds backward
  • Keep your neck relaxed and shoulders away from your ears throughout
Shoulder Rolls Warmup ►
10 forward, 10 backward
Mobilizes the scapulae and upper trapezius, releasing tension from desk work and preparing the shoulder girdle for the rowing and pressing movements ahead.
How To
  • Stand tall, arms relaxed at your sides
  • Roll both shoulders in a large circle — up, forward, down, back
  • 10 full rotations forward, then reverse for 10 backward
  • Make the circles as large as possible — exaggerate the movement
Torso Rotations Warmup ►
10 slow each side
Opens the thoracic spine for rotation — critical before rowing and pressing movements. Slow tempo is deliberate: fast rotations are momentum, not mobility.
How To
  • Stand with feet shoulder-width apart, arms crossed over chest or extended forward
  • Rotate your upper body slowly to one side as far as comfortable — hips stay forward
  • Pause briefly at end range, then rotate to the other side
  • Keep your core lightly braced and avoid letting your hips rotate with your torso
Wrist Circles Warmup ►
10 each direction
Prepares the wrist joints for the grip demands of the TRX handles and curl bar. Often skipped and often the source of unnecessary wrist discomfort during pushing exercises.
How To
  • Extend both arms in front of you, hands loose
  • Make slow deliberate circles with both wrists — 10 clockwise, 10 counterclockwise
  • Follow with 10 gentle wrist flexions and extensions to complete the range
Band Pull-Apart Warmup ►
15 reps
The single most important warmup movement for an upper body session. Activates the rear delts, rhomboids, and external rotators that stabilize the shoulder during every push and pull. If this burns quickly, you have the imbalance this program is designed to correct.
How To
  • Hold a light band at shoulder height, arms extended, hands shoulder-width apart
  • Pull the band apart by driving both arms out to your sides until band touches your chest
  • Squeeze your shoulder blades together at end range — hold 1 second
  • Return slowly — resist the band pulling your hands back together
  • Keep elbows straight and shoulders away from your ears throughout
Circuit × 3 Rounds — Rest 20s between exercises · Rest 60–90s between rounds
01 TRX Push-Up Moderate HR TRX ▶
10 reps · Adjust foot position for difficulty
Opens the session with the compound horizontal push. The instability of the TRX handles recruits your core throughout, making it more effective than a standard push-up. Moderate HR from the start — chest, front shoulders, triceps, and stabilizers all engaged simultaneously.
Form Cues
  • Grip handles with palms facing down, walk feet back until body is at a diagonal — more upright = easier, more horizontal = harder
  • Keep your body in a rigid plank from head to heels — do not let hips sag or pike up
  • Lower your chest toward the handles, elbows tracking at roughly 45° from your body — not flared wide
  • Press back to start, fully extending arms without locking elbows
  • Exhale on the push, inhale on the lower
02 Band Row Moderate HR Band (mid anchor) ▶
10 reps · Primary movers: mid-back, lats, rear delts
The horizontal pull paired with the push-up — no anchor change from the previous exercise. Together these two movements cover the full horizontal plane and balance the shoulder joint. Bands deliver peak resistance at peak contraction where your mid-back is strongest. HR stays elevated from the push-up.
Form Cues
  • Anchor band at mid-chest height. Stand facing the anchor, grip one end in each hand, step back until there is tension with arms extended.
  • Hinge slightly at the hips — about 15–20° forward lean — and maintain that position throughout the set
  • Pull both hands toward your lower ribs, driving elbows back and squeezing shoulder blades together at the top
  • Do not shrug — keep shoulders packed down away from your ears throughout
  • Control the return — resist the band pulling your arms forward; 2–3 counts on the way out
03 Band Pallof Press Active Recovery Band (mid anchor — no change) ▶
10 reps each side · Switch sides each round · Anti-rotation core work
Mid anchor stays set up from the row — no transition needed. Positioned here deliberately as an active recovery point between the two compound movements and the isolation work ahead. HR drops while core anti-rotation is trained — the obliques and deep stabilizers work without taxing the muscles just loaded. This is how your core actually functions in real life: resisting rotation, not generating it.
Form Cues
  • Stand side-on to the anchor, feet shoulder-width apart, hold the band with both hands at your sternum
  • Stand far enough from the anchor that there is meaningful tension in the band at your chest
  • Brace your core hard, then press both hands straight out in front of you until arms are extended
  • Hold for 2 seconds — resist the band trying to pull you toward the anchor
  • Return hands to your chest. That's one rep.
  • Your hips and shoulders should stay square throughout — no rotating toward or away from the anchor
  • Complete all reps on one side, then switch sides for the next round
04 Curl Bar Bicep Curl Low HR Curl Bar ▶
10 reps · Two-handed · EZ bar grip reduces wrist strain
Isolation work begins here — HR settles into its lower range for the back half of the circuit. The curl bar's angled grip reduces forearm strain while hitting both biceps simultaneously. Eccentric loading on the way down is where the strength adaptation happens most efficiently.
Form Cues
  • Grip the curl bar at the angled sections, hands roughly shoulder-width apart, palms facing up
  • Keep your elbows pinned to your sides throughout — they are the pivot point, not a lever to swing
  • Curl the bar up in a smooth arc toward your chest
  • Do not rock your body or use momentum — if you have to swing, rest and continue
  • Lower slowly — 3 counts down — this eccentric phase builds strength faster than rushing back to start
05 Band Tricep Pushdown Low HR Band (high anchor) ▶
12 reps · Primary movers: triceps
The tricep complements the bicep curl and closes out the arm work for this session. Anchor moves to high for the first time this circuit — one anchor change after three consecutive mid/curl bar exercises. Bands are effective here because resistance increases through the pushdown at exactly the point where the tricep has the most mechanical advantage.
Form Cues
  • Anchor band at high point. Stand facing the anchor, grip the band with both hands at chest height, palms facing down.
  • Keep your elbows pinned to your sides — they are the pivot point throughout the movement
  • Push the band down by extending your elbows until arms are fully straight
  • Squeeze your triceps hard at the bottom for 1 second
  • Let the band pull your hands back up slowly — do not just release — 2 counts on the way up
  • Slight forward lean is fine; rounding your back is not
06 Dead Bug HR Floor Bodyweight ▶
8 reps each side (16 total) · Slow and controlled
Circuit closer — HR at its floor, breathing should be controlled. The dead bug trains your deep core to resist spine extension while limbs move, reinforcing the same spinal bracing pattern used in the row and push-up. Ending the circuit on the floor with controlled breathing is an intentional gear-down before the inter-round rest.
Form Cues
  • Lie on your back, arms pointing straight up toward the ceiling, hips and knees at 90° (tabletop position)
  • Press your lower back firmly into the floor — eliminate the arch. Maintain this contact throughout every rep.
  • Brace your core, then slowly lower your right arm overhead and left leg toward the floor simultaneously
  • Lower only as far as you can without your lower back lifting off the floor
  • Return to start, then repeat on the opposite side — that's one rep each side
  • Exhale slowly throughout the lowering phase — this drives core engagement and keeps the lower back pinned
↔ Flexibility: Doorway Chest Stretch
30 sec each side · Between rounds
Stand in a doorway, place your forearm on the frame at 90°, and gently rotate your body away until you feel a stretch across your chest and front shoulder. Hold without bouncing. Critical for reversing forward shoulder posture that develops from pushing exercises and desk work. Switch sides each round.
Cool-Down — 2 min
Standing Forward Fold
30–45 sec
Stand with feet hip-width, hinge at hips and let your torso hang toward the floor. Bend knees slightly. Releases hamstrings and decompresses the lumbar spine.
TRX Chest Opener
30 sec
Face away from anchor, hold handles at hip height and walk forward slightly until you feel a gentle pull across chest. Arms slightly back, chin up. Counteracts the push-up chest fatigue and forward shoulder tension.

Warm-Up — 3–4 min
Hip Circles Warmup ►
10 each direction
Lubricates the hip joint and mobilizes the hip capsule before hinge-dominant work. The RDL and kettlebell swing both demand a well-mobilized hip — starting cold into a hinge pattern is the most common setup for lower back discomfort.
How To
  • Stand on one foot, hands on hips or holding a wall for balance
  • Draw large slow circles with your lifted knee — forward, out, back, in
  • 10 rotations each direction, each leg, making the circle as large as comfortable
  • Keep your standing leg slightly bent and torso upright throughout
Forward Leg Swings Warmup ►
10 each leg
Dynamic hamstring and hip flexor mobilization through their functional range. The swinging motion mimics the hip hinge pattern and prepares the posterior chain for the RDL without the compressive load of a static stretch.
How To
  • Stand sideways to a wall, one hand on the wall for balance
  • Swing the outside leg forward and back in a controlled pendulum — let momentum build gradually
  • The forward swing stretches the hamstring; the back swing opens the hip flexor
  • Don't force the range — let swing amplitude increase naturally over 10 reps
  • Complete all reps on one leg before switching sides
Lateral Leg Swings Warmup ►
10 each leg
Mobilizes hip abduction and adduction — the planes the lateral walk and glute work require. Also warms the adductors which are often the tightest structure in the lower body.
How To
  • Face a wall, both hands on the wall for balance
  • Swing one leg across your body and back out to the side in a controlled arc
  • Let the swing amplitude gradually increase — don't force range on the first rep
  • Keep your hips level and avoid leaning your torso to compensate
Bodyweight RDL Warmup ►
8 slow reps
Grooves the hip hinge pattern before adding any load. The most important warmup movement for this session — if the pattern is not clean here, it will not be clean under load. Use this as a diagnostic: feel where your hamstrings engage and where your lower back wants to round.
How To
  • Stand with feet hip-width apart, arms hanging in front of your thighs
  • Push your hips back — not down. Imagine closing a car door with your backside.
  • Let your hands travel down the front of your legs as your hips move back
  • Maintain a neutral spine — feel the hamstring stretch, not the lower back
  • Drive hips forward to stand, squeeze glutes at the top
  • Pause 1 second at top and bottom — this is about pattern, not speed
Standing Calf Raises Warmup ►
15 reps bodyweight, full range
Increases blood flow to the lower leg and ankles before the lunge and calf work. Use this to assess ankle mobility before loading it — if the heel rises early on the way down, that is the limiting factor.
How To
  • Stand with feet hip-width, hands lightly touching a wall for balance only
  • Rise all the way up onto the balls of your feet
  • Lower all the way down — heels at or below floor level if on a step
  • Slow tempo — 2 counts up, 2 counts down. This is mobility prep, not a working set.
Circuit × 3 Rounds — Rest 20s between exercises · Rest 60–90s between rounds
01 Curl Bar Romanian Deadlift HR Builds Curl Bar ▶
10 reps · Primary movers: hamstrings, glutes, lower back
Opens the session by establishing the hip hinge pattern — the foundation of everything that follows. HR builds from the RDL as the large posterior chain muscles engage. The warm-up bodyweight RDL means you're grooved and ready to add load. The curl bar tracks close to the legs naturally, which is correct RDL mechanics.
Form Cues
  • Stand with feet hip-width apart, grip the curl bar with both hands at center, bar hanging in front of your thighs, palms facing your body
  • Push your hips back — not down. This is a hinge, not a squat. Imagine closing a car door with your backside.
  • Keep the bar close to your legs as it travels down — it should nearly graze your shins
  • Maintain a neutral spine throughout — do not round the lower back
  • Lower until you feel a stretch in your hamstrings — typically just below the knee
  • Drive hips forward to return to standing, squeezing glutes at the top
02 Kettlebell Swing HR Spike 10–12 lb KB ▶
10–12 reps · Primary movers: glutes, hamstrings, upper traps, posterior chain
The HR spike of the session — placed immediately after the RDL while the hip hinge pattern is fresh and warmed up. The explosive hip drive is the same mechanics as the RDL, now performed with power. This is the only truly high-HR exercise in the program and it does significant cardiovascular work in a short window. The upper traps fire dynamically to control the bell — functional upper back load without a separate exercise.
Form Cues
  • Stand with feet slightly wider than hip-width, kettlebell on the floor about a foot in front of you
  • Hinge at the hips to grip the bell — same pattern as the RDL. Tilt it toward you, hike it back between your legs to load the hinge.
  • Drive your hips forward explosively — the power comes entirely from the hip snap, not your arms
  • Let the bell float to chest/shoulder height — do not muscle it up with your arms or shrug at the top
  • As the bell descends, hinge your hips back to absorb it — redirect into the next rep
  • Keep your back flat throughout — if you feel it in your lower back, you're squatting the movement instead of hinging it
03 TRX Reverse Lunge HR Elevated TRX ▶
8 reps each leg (16 total) · TRX for balance only
HR comes off the swing spike but stays elevated as the largest compound lower body movement of the session begins. Trains each leg independently, exposing and correcting strength imbalances. The TRX handles provide balance assistance — grip them as lightly as possible and rely on them less as confidence grows. Quad and glute demand is high here.
Form Cues
  • Hold TRX handles at hip height, stand with feet together
  • Step one foot back, lowering that knee toward the floor — the front foot stays planted
  • Front shin should remain roughly vertical — knee should not shoot far past your toes
  • Lower until back knee is 1–2 inches from the floor, then drive through the front heel to return
  • Keep your torso tall and upright throughout — do not lean forward
  • Use the TRX for balance only — the leg does the work
  • Right knee: monitor deep flexion, reduce depth before reducing load if discomfort arises
04 Band Hamstring Curl HR Descends Band (low anchor) ▶
10 reps · 2-second pause at peak contraction
Transitions to floor work — HR begins its controlled descent. The RDL trains hamstrings in a lengthened position via hip hinge; this trains them in a shortened position via knee flexion — the hamstring's other primary function. Together they give complete hamstring stimulus and protect the knee from imbalances that cause strains. The 2-second pause at peak contraction is the progressive overload variable for this session.
Form Cues
  • Anchor band at floor level. Loop or attach around one ankle. Lie face down with working leg extended toward anchor.
  • Curl your heel toward your glutes — the band gets harder as you approach full contraction
  • Hold at the top for 2 full seconds
  • Lower slowly — resist the band pulling your leg back; 3 counts down
  • Complete all reps on one leg, then switch. Keep hips flat against the floor — no rotation.
05 Band Glute Bridge Low HR Band (looped above knees) ▶
15 reps · Drive through heels · Squeeze at top
Stays on the floor from the hamstring curl — band simply moves from ankle to above knees. Directly targets the glutes through hip extension, with the band above the knees adding an abduction component that activates the glute medius. Together with the swing and lunge this gives the glutes three distinct stimuli in one session — hip drive, unilateral load, and isolated bridge extension.
Form Cues
  • Loop a band just above your knees. Lie on your back, knees bent, feet flat on the floor hip-width apart.
  • Push your knees outward against the band — keep this outward pressure throughout the entire movement
  • Drive through your heels and lift your hips until your body forms a straight line from knees to shoulders
  • Squeeze your glutes hard at the top — pause for 1–2 seconds
  • Lower slowly — 2–3 counts down
  • Keep your lower back from overarching at the top — posterior pelvic tilt slightly
06 Standard Plank HR Floor Bodyweight ▶
30–40 sec hold · Focus on form over duration
Circuit closer — HR at its floor, controlled breathing. Core anti-extension work to complement the hip hinge and lunge patterns of the session. The plank trains the same spinal bracing you used under load in the RDL, now held isometrically. Breathe steadily throughout — holding your breath spikes blood pressure unnecessarily.
Form Cues
  • Forearms on the floor, elbows directly under shoulders, forearms parallel or hands clasped
  • Body should form a straight line from head to heels — no sagging hips, no piked hips
  • Squeeze your glutes, brace your abs as if bracing for a punch — hold that contraction throughout
  • Look at the floor, neutral neck — do not crane your head up
  • Breathe steadily — slow nasal inhale and exhale throughout
  • If form breaks before time is up, rest 5 seconds and resume rather than holding poor position
↔ Flexibility: TRX Hip Flexor Stretch
30 sec each side · Between rounds
In a kneeling lunge position, hold the TRX handles for support and shift your hips slightly forward, keeping your torso tall. Deep stretch in the front of the rear hip. Critical after the reverse lunge pattern — hip flexors get loaded and shortened during lunges and need active lengthening between rounds.
Cool-Down — 2–3 min
Figure-4 Stretch ⚠ Right knee: this version only — avoid pigeon pose
60 sec each side
Lie on your back, cross one ankle over the opposite knee, and pull both legs toward your chest. Deep glute and hip external rotator stretch. Essential after the lunge and glute bridge pattern. The figure-4 version is safer than pigeon pose for the right knee and equally effective for the target tissue.
Standing Forward Fold
30–45 sec
Stand with feet hip-width, hinge at hips and let your torso hang toward the floor. Bend knees slightly. Releases hamstrings and decompresses the lumbar spine after the heavy hinge and lunge work of this session.

Warm-Up — 3–4 min
Arm Circles Warmup ►
15 sec forward, 15 sec backward
Lubricates the shoulder joint before the vertical pulling and pressing demands of this session. The lat pulldown and overhead press both require a well-mobilized shoulder capsule.
How To
  • Stand with feet hip-width apart, arms extended straight out to your sides
  • Draw circles with your fingertips — start small and progressively widen to full range
  • 15 seconds forward, then immediately reverse for 15 seconds backward
  • Keep shoulders away from your ears and neck relaxed throughout
Shoulder Rolls Warmup ►
10 forward, 10 backward
Mobilizes the scapulae before the vertical pulling work ahead. The lat pulldown and face pull both require scapular depression and retraction — this gets those muscles firing before the load arrives.
How To
  • Stand tall, arms relaxed at your sides
  • Roll both shoulders in a large circle — up, forward, down, back
  • 10 full rotations forward, then reverse for 10 backward
  • Exaggerate the movement — make the circles as large as possible
Overhead Reach with Side Bend Warmup ►
5 each side
Opens the lateral line — lats, obliques, and intercostals — before overhead pressing. If the lat is tight, the overhead press compensates by arching the lower back. This prevents that.
How To
  • Stand with feet shoulder-width apart
  • Reach one arm straight overhead, then side-bend away from that arm
  • Hold 2–3 seconds at end range — feel the stretch from hip to fingertips
  • Return to standing and repeat on the other side
  • Keep the reaching arm close to your ear — don't let it drift forward
Band Pull-Apart Warmup ►
15 reps
Critical activation before the vertical pulling work in this session. The lat pulldown and face pull rely on rear delt and lower trap function — this ensures they are firing before the load arrives rather than the biceps and upper traps compensating.
How To
  • Hold a light band at shoulder height, arms extended, hands shoulder-width apart
  • Pull the band apart driving both arms out to your sides until band touches your chest
  • Squeeze shoulder blades together at end range — hold 1 second
  • Return slowly — resist the band pulling your hands back together
  • Keep elbows straight and shoulders away from your ears throughout
Wrist Circles Warmup ►
10 each direction
Prepares the wrists for the grip demands of the lat pulldown and overhead press. The overhead position places the wrist in extension under load — a few circles prevents the sharp discomfort that catches people off guard.
How To
  • Extend both arms in front of you, hands loose
  • Make slow deliberate circles — 10 clockwise, 10 counterclockwise
  • Follow with 10 gentle wrist flexions and extensions

01 Band Lat Pulldown Moderate HR Band (high anchor) ▶
10 reps · Primary movers: lats, lower traps, biceps
Opens with the vertical pull — the movement pattern with no coverage in Upper 1. Pulling down from overhead targets the lats through their full range, which is not achievable from a horizontal pull. The quarter-squat hold position means your legs are also working isometrically, which contributes modestly to HR from the start.
Form Cues
  • Anchor band at maximum height. Grip one end in each hand, arms extended overhead.
  • Take a step or two back from the anchor to create diagonal band tension, feet shoulder-width apart
  • Sit into a quarter-squat position and hold it throughout the set
  • Pull the band down toward your chest by driving your elbows down and back — think "elbows to hip pockets"
  • At the bottom, squeeze your lats hard — you should feel the contraction in the sides of your back
  • Return with control — fully extend arms overhead to complete lat stretch at the top of each rep
02 Band Face Pull Moderate HR Band (high anchor — no change) ▶
12 reps · Primary movers: rear delts, lower traps, external rotators
High anchor stays set — no transition. Where the lat pulldown pulls vertically to target the lats, the face pull pulls horizontally-high to target the rear delts and external rotators — complementary angles on the same posterior chain. Together these two exercises give the upper back complete stimulus. The external rotation finish is what differentiates this from a simple rear delt exercise and makes it therapeutically important for shoulder health.
Form Cues
  • Grip one end in each hand, palms facing down, thumbs toward you. Step back until there's tension with arms extended.
  • Pull the band toward your face, leading with your elbows — elbows should flare out and up, finishing above shoulder height
  • At full pull, your hands should be beside your ears — like a double bicep pose
  • The key finish: externally rotate — pull your knuckles back so your palms face forward. This activates the lower traps and rotator cuff.
  • Return slowly with control — do not let the band snap your arms forward
03 Band Overhead Press Moderate HR Band (low anchor) ▶
12 reps · Primary movers: shoulders, triceps, upper traps
Anchor drops to low — one anchor change. The vertical push that complements the vertical pull of the lat pulldown. Three consecutive compound movements keeps HR elevated through the first half of the circuit. The low anchor means resistance increases as you press overhead, which matches the shoulder's strength curve better than dumbbells at this range of motion.
Form Cues
  • Anchor band at low point. Stand facing away from the anchor, hold the band at shoulder height with palms facing forward.
  • Press directly overhead until arms are fully extended
  • Keep your core braced — do not arch your lower back or lean back as you press overhead
  • Avoid shrugging your shoulders at the top — keep them packed down even at full extension
  • Lower with control — 2 counts down — resist the band on the way back
04 Band Lateral Raise HR Descends Band (low anchor — no change) ▶
10 reps · Controlled tempo — no swinging
Low anchor stays set — no transition. HR begins its descent into isolation work. Targets the medial deltoid, which gives the shoulder its width and roundness. This muscle has no coverage in Upper 1 — it belongs in Upper 2 specifically because the lateral raise is a controlled, lower-intensity exercise that fits the descending arc of this circuit well.
Form Cues
  • Stand with feet hip-width, grip band ends at your sides, slight bend at the elbow
  • Raise both arms simultaneously out to the sides until they reach shoulder height — no higher
  • Lead with your elbows, not your hands — think of pouring water out of a pitcher at the top
  • Do not shrug — keep shoulders packed down as the arms rise
  • No momentum or body rocking — if you have to swing, reduce band tension
  • Lower slowly — 3 counts down
05 Band Bicep Curl Low HR Band (low anchor — no change) ▶
10 reps · Primary movers: biceps brachii
Low anchor unchanged — zero transition from the lateral raise. Second bicep stimulus of the week, different implement from Upper 1's curl bar. Band delivers peak tension at peak contraction where the bicep is strongest, complementing the curl bar version. Three consecutive low anchor exercises in a row means the second half of this circuit flows without any setup changes.
Form Cues
  • Stand facing the anchor, grip one end in each hand, palms facing forward, arms extended toward anchor with slight tension
  • Keep your elbows pinned to your sides throughout
  • Curl both hands up toward your shoulders — the band gets progressively harder as you approach the top
  • Squeeze hard at peak contraction for 1 second before lowering
  • Lower slowly — 3 counts down — resist the band pulling your hands back down
06 Standard Plank HR Floor Bodyweight ▶
30–40 sec hold · Controlled breathing throughout
Circuit closer — HR at floor, controlled breathing. Core stability work closes both upper sessions. The plank here reinforces the shoulder and scapular stability that the pressing and pulling movements require. Maintaining shoulder pack and core brace in a static position consolidates the neuromuscular patterns from the session.
Form Cues
  • Forearms on the floor, elbows directly under shoulders
  • Body should form a straight line from head to heels — no sagging hips, no piked hips
  • Squeeze glutes, brace abs — hold that contraction throughout
  • Look at the floor, neutral neck
  • Breathe steadily — slow nasal inhale and exhale
  • If form breaks before time is up, rest 5 seconds and resume

Bodyweight Sumo Squat Warmup ►
8 slow reps
Primes the wide-stance pattern and inner thigh before the adductor-dominant work in this session. Use this to find your comfortable sumo stance width before adding any resistance.
How To

01 Band Lateral Walk Moderate HR Band (looped above knees) ▶
12 steps each direction · Stay in athletic position throughout
Opens the session standing with the most cardiovascularly demanding exercise in this circuit — establishes HR before moving to the floor. The glute medius is the primary target: chronically undertrained and directly responsible for knee tracking, hip stability, and lower back health. The lateral walk loads it through its primary function — hip abduction under load. The band's return phase simultaneously loads the adductors.
Form Cues
  • Loop band just above knees. Stand with feet hip-width, slight bend in knees, slight forward lean from hips — athletic stance.
  • Step sideways with your lead foot, then follow with the trail foot — keep band taut throughout, never let feet come together completely
  • Keep your hips low and level — do not bob up and down with each step
  • You should feel the outer glute of the stepping leg; the adductor of the returning leg on the close step
  • 12 steps right, then 12 steps left = 1 set. Stay in the athletic position the entire time.
02 Band Adductor Side-Lying Squeeze HR Drops Band (loops above knees — no change) ▶
15 reps each side · Slow squeeze, slow release
Loops stay on from the lateral walk — move directly to the floor. Direct adductor isolation after the compound lateral walk loaded them in their secondary role. Building adductor baseline strength is particularly important at 50+ when connective tissue recovery slows and groin strains become more costly. HR drops intentionally here — this is the active recovery point of this session before the hip flexor work ahead.
Form Cues
  • Lie on your side with band looped just above your knees. Stack hips directly on top of each other — don't roll back.
  • Bottom leg is the working leg. Top leg rests elevated on a pillow or folded towel.
  • Lift the bottom leg up toward the top leg, squeezing your inner thigh to initiate the movement
  • Pause at the top for 1 second, then lower with control
  • Complete all reps on one side, then roll over and repeat
  • If the band makes this too difficult initially, do it without the band — bodyweight is sufficient to start
03 Standing Hip Flexor March Low HR Band (low anchor) ▶
12 reps each leg · Slow and deliberate
One anchor change — loops off, band moves to low anchor. Hip flexors are stretched throughout the program but this is their only direct strengthening work. Weak hip flexors contribute to lower back pain, poor posture, and reduced stride efficiency. The slow deliberate tempo here is intentional — this is strength work for an underserved muscle, not a cardio exercise.
Form Cues
  • Anchor a band at floor level. Loop it around your ankle or secure it to your foot.
  • Stand upright facing away from the anchor, slight tension in the band with the working leg extended back
  • Drive your knee upward toward your chest — aim for thigh parallel to the floor or higher
  • Hold the top position for 1 full second — you should feel the front of your hip working
  • Lower with control — do not let the band yank your leg back; resist it on the way down
  • Keep your standing leg slightly bent and your torso tall — do not lean back to compensate
04 Band Glute Bridge Low HR Band (looped above knees) ▶
15 reps · Drive through heels · Squeeze at top
Loops back above the knees — move to the floor. Second weekly glute bridge stimulus, appearing in both lower sessions for frequency. Glutes recover well and respond to twice-weekly direct work. On this day the bridge follows the lateral walk and adductor work, so the glute medius is already activated — the bridge here will feel more connected than when performed cold.
Form Cues
  • Loop a band just above your knees. Lie on your back, knees bent, feet flat on the floor hip-width apart.
  • Push your knees outward against the band throughout the entire movement
  • Drive through your heels and lift your hips until your body forms a straight line from knees to shoulders
  • Squeeze your glutes hard at the top — pause for 1–2 seconds
  • Lower slowly — 2–3 counts down
05 Side Plank HR Floor Bodyweight ▶
25–35 sec each side · Switch sides each round
The lateral stability plane — the one core direction not covered by the standard plank or dead bug. Trains the quadratus lumborum and obliques to resist lateral spinal collapse, and places meaningful demand on the glute medius of the bottom leg — a second stimulus for the same muscle targeted by the lateral walk. 01 World's Greatest Stretch Full body Bodyweight ►
5 each side
The single best full-body mobility exercise. Lunge plus thoracic rotation plus hip flexor stretch in one movement. Directly addresses the hip and thoracic demands created by the lunges, RDLs, and rows in the resistance sessions.
How To
  • Step into a deep lunge, front foot flat, back knee hovering
  • Place same-side hand as front foot on the floor inside your front foot
  • Rotate your top arm up toward the ceiling, following it with your eyes
  • Hold 2 seconds at the top of the rotation, then return hand to floor
  • Shift hips back slightly to feel the hamstring stretch, then return to lunge
  • That is one rep — step through and repeat on the other side
02 Inchworm Full body Bodyweight ►
6 slow reps
Hamstring flexibility, shoulder stability, and spinal articulation in one movement. Warms the posterior chain without loading it. The 2-second plank hold makes this active mobility work rather than just a stretch.
How To
  • Stand with feet hip-width, hinge at hips and place hands on the floor
  • Walk your hands forward until you are in a full plank — hold 2 seconds
  • Keep your core braced in the plank — no sagging hips
  • Walk your hands back toward your feet, keeping legs as straight as comfortable
  • Stand up and repeat — slow and deliberate, not a race
03 Figure-4 with Active Press Hip external rotators Bodyweight ►
5 contract-relax cycles each side
Stage 2 of the hip mobility progression toward 90/90. The contract-relax (PNF) technique produces faster range of motion gains than passive stretching alone — the 5-second active press followed by a passive sink is the mechanism. Right knee: this version only throughout the program.
How To
  • Lie on your back, cross one ankle over the opposite knee in the figure-4 position
  • Pull both legs gently toward your chest until you feel a stretch in the outer glute
  • Press the crossed knee away from your chest against your hand for 5 seconds — resist it
  • After 5 seconds, relax and gently pull legs closer to chest — you should gain range
  • That is one cycle. Repeat 5 times per side.
  • Right knee: this version only — never pigeon pose
04 Seated Hip CARs Hip joint — full range Bodyweight ►
5 rotations each direction each leg
Controlled Articular Rotations — builds active control through the hip's full range, not just passive flexibility. This is what the 90/90 position actually requires: range you can control, not just range you can fall into. The bridge between passive stretching and active hip mobility.
How To
  • Sit on the floor with both legs extended, hands behind you for support
  • Lift one leg slightly, bend the knee, and slowly draw the largest circle possible with that knee
  • Forward phase: bring knee toward chest, then rotate outward
  • Backward phase: take the knee out wide, then rotate inward and back to start
  • Go as slowly as possible — speed defeats the purpose of CARs
  • 5 circles each direction, each leg — feel every degree of the range
05 Supported 90/90 Hold Hip external + internal rotation Bodyweight ►
30 sec each side
Stage 4 of the hip mobility progression — accessing the 90/90 position with support before attempting to sit upright. Hands stay on the floor behind you. Focus on keeping both sitting bones on the floor, not on the shin angle. Over weeks this hold becomes easier and the hands come off the floor.
How To

  • Front leg: shin perpendicular to your body; back leg: shin parallel behind you
  • Place both hands on the floor behind you for support — lean back slightly if needed
  • Focus on keeping both sitting bones on the floor — do not force the shin angle
  • Breathe slowly and let gravity do the work — do not push into pain

12 Overhead Lat Stretch Lats + thoracic Bodyweight ►
60 sec each side
Directly addresses the tightness created by lat pulldowns and rows — often missed entirely in cool-downs. A tight lat limits overhead pressing range and pulls the shoulder into internal rotation, which is the postural problem the face pulls and band pull-aparts are trying to correct. Stretching it directly closes that loop.
How To
  • Grab a doorframe or TRX handle at shoulder height with one hand
  • Step back and lean away until you feel a stretch through the side of your back
  • Let your body weight create the stretch — do not force it
  • Keep the arm straight and allow the lat to lengthen under gravity
  • 60 seconds per side — the lat is a large muscle and needs time
13 Supine Spinal Twist Lumbar spine + thoracic Bodyweight ►
60 sec each side
Rotational decompression for the lumbar spine. The resistance sessions place significant anti-rotation demand on the spine throughout — the plank, dead bug, and Pallof press all train the spine to resist rotation under load. This releases that accumulated tension through passive rotation in the opposite direction.
How To
  • Lie on your back with arms extended wide — T position
  • Pull one knee across your body and let it fall toward the floor on the opposite side
  • Let gravity do the work — do not force the knee down
  • Keep both shoulders on the floor — if one lifts, reduce the knee angle
  • Look away from the bent knee for a full spinal rotation
  • 60 seconds per side
14 Calf Ankle Stretch Series Gastrocnemius + soleus Bodyweight ►
45 sec each position each leg
Two-position stretch because the calf has two muscles with different knee-angle dependencies. The gastrocnemius crosses the knee and requires a straight leg to stretch fully. The soleus does not cross the knee and requires a bent knee. Doing only one position means half the calf is never stretched.
How To
  • Position 1 — Straight leg (gastrocnemius): step one foot back, press heel into floor, keep back leg straight, lean gently forward
  • Position 2 — Bent knee (soleus): same foot placement but bend the back knee slightly — the stretch moves lower into the ankle
  • Hold each position 45 seconds before switching
  • Press the heel actively into the floor throughout — do not let it float
Program Notes
Upper/Lower Recovery Logic
Each tissue type gets 48+ hours of recovery between sessions of the same type. Upper 1 and Upper 2 are never run on consecutive days; same rule for Lower 1 and Lower 2. Upper/Lower or Lower/Upper back-to-back is fine. On a 3-session week the rotation shifts by one — over 4 weeks every session gets equal exposure.
HR Curve Design
Each session is designed with a deliberate HR arc. Lower 1 has the strongest cardiovascular effect — the kettlebell swing is the program's only high-HR explosive movement. Upper sessions peak at moderate HR. The Pallof Press (Upper 1) and Adductor Squeeze (Lower 2) are intentional active recovery points mid-circuit, not fillers.
Scaling — TRX
Move feet forward (more upright) to make easier. Move feet back (more horizontal) to increase resistance. No equipment changes needed — this is your primary progression tool for the first 4–6 weeks on TRX movements.
Scaling — Bands
Choke up (grip closer to anchor) for more resistance. Use more length for less. Double the band for more resistance on any exercise. Bands increase resistance at end range — expect the hardest point to be at peak contraction.
Heart Rate Targets
Observed range: floor ~120 BPM, peaks ~150 BPM. This is above the original target and reflects productive EPOC-generating intensity. Max HR estimate at 50: ~170 BPM. If consistently exceeding 155, extend inter-round rest to 90 sec. Lower 1 will peak highest due to the kettlebell swing.
Session Timing
Sessions are designed to run ~40 minutes including warm-up and cool-down. Do not compress rest periods to shorten sessions — the 60–90 second inter-round rest is a deliberate HR management tool, not optional recovery. Rushing rest periods defeats the cardiovascular design of the circuits.
Right Knee — Mobility Note
Right knee has some mobility limitation. Pigeon pose is contraindicated — substitute Figure-4 stretch (supine, cross ankle over knee, pull toward chest) throughout. Monitor deep knee flexion in lunges; use TRX for balance assistance and reduce depth before reducing load if discomfort arises.
Cardiovascular Risk Context
Risk factors: cholesterol, family history, weight. The circuit format and elevated HR are intentional therapeutic choices. Resistance training independently improves lipid profiles, insulin sensitivity, and vascular function. Complement with physician monitoring of lipid panels and blood pressure.
